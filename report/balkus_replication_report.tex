\section{Background}
%======================================================================

When we use machine learning to estimate causal effects, the ML model can overfit: it learns patterns in the training data that don't generalize. This creates \emph{empirical process (EP) bias} --- a systematic error in the treatment effect estimate caused by using the same data for both nuisance estimation and inference. \emph{Cross-fitting} fixes this by splitting the data in two --- train the ML model on one half, estimate the causal effect on the other half, then swap \citep{chernozhukov2018}.

But what if units in the data are correlated --- e.g., students nested in schools, or workers nested in firms? \citet{chiang2021} argue that splits must \emph{respect} the clustering: if two observations share a cluster, they cannot be in different folds. They propose K$^2$-fold splitting, which partitions both row and column clusters into $K$ groups. The downside: with $K=2$, each training fold sees only $\sim$25\% of the data.

\citet{balkus2026} prove this elaborate splitting is \textbf{usually unnecessary}. Their Theorem~1 shows that plain random splitting --- ignoring clusters entirely --- still eliminates the EP bias, as long as cluster sizes don't grow with the sample. They call this ``as-IID'' splitting.

There is one caveat. Balkus note that even though the \emph{splitting strategy} doesn't need to know about clusters, the \emph{standard errors} still do:
\begin{quote}
\emph{``Variance estimates used for the construction of confidence intervals must be adjusted to account for inter-unit correlation.''} --- \citet[Section~4]{balkus2026}
\end{quote}

This distinction --- splitting strategy vs.\ standard error formula --- is the central theme of this report.

\paragraph{Three strategies we compare.}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em, nosep]
  \item \textbf{No CF}: No splitting at all. Train and predict on the same data. This is the baseline that shows when cross-fitting matters.
  \item \textbf{As-IID}: Standard random $K$-fold splitting, ignoring clusters.
  \item \textbf{Chiang K$^2$-fold}: Cluster-aware splitting that guarantees statistical independence between training and test data.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{A note on standard errors.} Chiang et al.\ propose a ``multiway cluster robust standard error'' alongside their K$^2$-fold splitting. This formula is the classical two-way cluster-robust variance $\hat{V} = V_{\text{row}} + V_{\text{col}} - V_{\text{iid}}$ from \citet{cameron2011}, applied to the DML influence function scores $\psi_i$. Chiang's contribution is not a new formula, but the \emph{theoretical proof} that Cameron et al.'s formula remains valid under their specific cross-fitting scheme with dependent data. In our implementation, we use this same formula uniformly across all three strategies. This lets us attribute any coverage difference to the splitting strategy alone, without confounding it with the SE estimator.

\paragraph{Why not use the DoubleML library?} The standard \texttt{DoubleML} Python library bundles the splitting strategy and the variance formula into a single choice: feeding it a \texttt{DoubleMLClusterData} object gives you cluster-aware K$^2$-fold splitting \emph{and} cluster-robust SEs together; feeding it plain \texttt{DoubleMLData} gives you as-IID splitting \emph{and} IID SEs. There is no way to request the combination Balkus recommends --- as-IID splitting with cluster-robust SEs --- through their API. For this reason, we translated Balkus's R code (\texttt{hard\_cluster.R}) into Python line by line: the AIPW score function, the two fold generators (\texttt{iid\_cf} and \texttt{make\_multi\_folds}), and the IID SE formula all match their R equivalents exactly. On top of this faithful translation, we added the Cameron et al.\ (2011) cluster-robust variance formula and two additional learners (Deep RF and Lasso). This gives us a $3 \times 2$ factorial design (strategy $\times$ SE type) that the \texttt{DoubleML} API cannot produce. Our Balkus estimator is validated by matching the published MARS coverage results from their R code; our cluster-robust SE formula is separately validated against the \texttt{DoubleML} library on Chiang's own DGP (Section~\ref{sec:chiang}).

\section{How We Measure Success}
\label{sec:morris}
%======================================================================

We run each experiment 200 times with different random seeds. Each run produces two numbers: a treatment effect estimate $\hat{\theta}$ and a standard error $\widehat{SE}$ (what the formula \emph{thinks} the uncertainty is). Because we control the data-generating process, we know the true treatment effect $\theta_0$.

Following \citet{morris2019}, we compute:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em, nosep]
  \item \textbf{Bias}: How far off are the estimates on average? ($\bar{\hat{\theta}} - \theta_0$)
  \item \textbf{EmpSE}: The \emph{true} uncertainty, measured as the standard deviation of the 200 estimates.
  \item \textbf{ModSE}: The \emph{reported} uncertainty, computed as $\sqrt{\text{mean}(\widehat{SE}^2)}$ across the 200 runs.
  \item \textbf{RelSE = ModSE / EmpSE}: Are the reported standard errors correct?
  \item \textbf{Coverage}: How often does the 95\% confidence interval contain the true $\theta_0$?
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Why RelSE matters more than coverage.} Two methods can both have 88\% coverage, but one might have RelSE $= 0.90$ (SEs are slightly too small --- fixable) while the other has RelSE $= 0.39$ (SEs are catastrophically wrong --- the method is fundamentally broken). Coverage hides this difference. RelSE reveals it.

\section{Balkus Replication: Which Learner Needs Cross-Fitting?}
\label{sec:balkus}
%======================================================================

We test three ML learners on Balkus's two-way clustered DGP. Each learner sits at a different point on the ``overfitting spectrum'':

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em, nosep]
  \item \textbf{MARS} (R's \texttt{earth}): Additive splines with built-in pruning. A ``careful'' learner that avoids overfitting by design.
  \item \textbf{Deep RF} (sklearn): Fully-grown random forests that memorize the training data. A ``reckless'' learner --- exactly the kind cross-fitting was invented for.
  \item \textbf{Lasso}: L1-regularized regression. Not technically Donsker, but L1 regularization keeps it from overfitting enough to create large EP bias --- it \emph{behaves} like a careful learner.
\end{itemize}

All results in this section use Balkus's original DGP (a direct replication of their R code in Python). We also explored harder DGP variants --- removing propensity compression, introducing shared confounders, and adversarial cluster structures --- but defer those results to future work as their implications require further investigation.


\subsection{The Key Finding: MARS Doesn't Need Cross-Fitting At All}

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{replications/balkus/results/plots/mars_vs_deep_rf.png}
\caption{Coverage by learner and strategy on the Balkus Original DGP (averaged over three sample sizes).}
\label{fig:mars-vs-rf}
\end{figure}

MARS achieves 89--92\% coverage \emph{without any cross-fitting}. Its built-in GCV pruning prevents the overfitting that creates EP bias. As \citet[p.~C16]{chernozhukov2018} note: if the learner belongs to a ``Donsker class'' (roughly: it doesn't overfit enough to create EP bias), cross-fitting is unnecessary. MARS is approximately Donsker; Lasso is not technically Donsker, but L1 regularization keeps its EP bias small enough that it behaves similarly.

This creates a problem: \textbf{MARS is the wrong learner to test Balkus's theorem.} If cross-fitting doesn't matter regardless, we can't tell whether as-IID splitting ``works'' or whether splitting is just irrelevant. That's why we introduced Deep RF --- a learner where cross-fitting genuinely matters.

\paragraph{Deep RF confirms Balkus.} Without cross-fitting, Deep RF creates massive EP bias: coverage drops to 45\%. With as-IID splitting, the EP bias vanishes and coverage recovers to 88\%. K$^2$-fold also helps (78\%) but with lower coverage due to data starvation.


\subsection{The SE Calibration Story}

Coverage tells you \emph{whether} your confidence intervals work. RelSE tells you \emph{why} they fail. Figure~\ref{fig:morris-relse} shows both IID and cluster-robust (CR) SE calibration side by side.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{replications/balkus/results/plots/morris_relse_dual.png}
\caption{Morris et al.\ (2019) SE calibration on the Balkus Original DGP. RelSE close to 1.0 (green zone) means well-calibrated SEs. Left: IID SEs. Right: cluster-robust SEs (Cameron et al., 2011).}
\label{fig:morris-relse}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{What the left panel shows (IID SEs):}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em, nosep]
  \item \textbf{Deep RF without CF is broken.} RelSE $= 0.39$ means the reported SEs are only 39\% of the true uncertainty.
  \item \textbf{Cross-fitting fixes it.} With as-IID, RelSE jumps to $0.91$. K$^2$-fold also helps ($0.85$) but less so, due to smaller training sets.
  \item \textbf{MARS and Lasso don't need cross-fitting for EP bias.} Their RelSE is already 0.82--0.87 without CF. As-IID improves them to 0.85--0.91.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{What the right panel adds (CR SEs):}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em, nosep]
  \item \textbf{Lasso as-IID + CR SE: 0.98} --- nearly perfect calibration. This is the best combination in the entire figure.
  \item \textbf{MARS no-CF + CR SE: 0.90} --- CR SEs bring MARS into the calibrated zone even without cross-fitting. MARS's influence function has mild cluster correlation that IID SEs miss.
  \item \textbf{Deep RF no-CF + CR SE: 0.63} --- CR SEs help (up from 0.39) but cannot fix EP bias alone.
  \item \textbf{Deep RF as-IID + CR SE: 0.92} --- the best Deep RF combination. Cross-fitting removes the EP bias; CR SEs account for residual cluster correlation.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Bottom line:} Cross-fitting eliminates EP bias (Chernozhukov's contribution). CR SEs account for remaining cluster correlation. For careful learners (MARS, Lasso), CR SEs alone nearly suffice. For reckless learners (Deep RF), you need \emph{both}.

\section{Chiang Replication: Testing on Chiang's Own DGP}
\label{sec:chiang}
%======================================================================

Everything above uses Balkus's AIPW estimator. A natural question: do the results hold on a completely different estimator?

We replicate the partially linear IV (PLIV) model from \citet[Section~4.1]{chiang2021}. This is their own simulation design with Lasso as the first-stage learner and $p = 100$ covariates, so it's the fairest possible test. Lasso is not technically Donsker, but its L1 regularization keeps EP bias small --- it \emph{behaves} like a Donsker-class learner in practice. The Chiang replication therefore tests \emph{data starvation} and \emph{decoupling} rather than EP bias removal. We deliberately keep Lasso here: the non-Donsker case (Deep RF) was already settled comprehensively in Section~\ref{sec:balkus}.

For our argument --- that as-IID + CR SE dominates K$^2$-fold regardless of SE formula --- the conclusion is already clear.

Balkus's theorem tells us these are \emph{logically independent} choices. The optimal combination --- as-IID splitting with cluster-robust SEs --- is the one row DoubleML doesn't offer.

\section{Where the Theory Breaks}
\label{sec:boundary}
%======================================================================

Balkus's Corollary~2 requires that cluster sizes are \emph{bounded} --- each row contains at most $C$ columns, where $C$ doesn't grow with the sample. In a full $N \times M$ grid, every row contains $M$ observations, so $C = M \to \infty$. We tested this with strong cluster effects. Coverage declines from 85\% to 71\% as the grid grows --- for \emph{both} strategies equally. The culprit is the cluster-robust SE formula itself (which assumes fixed cluster sizes), not the splitting strategy. On bounded-cluster DGPs (sparse bipartite graph, $d=20$), both strategies achieve 97\% coverage, confirming the boundary is exactly where the theory predicts.

\section{Conclusion}
%======================================================================

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em, nosep]
  \item \textbf{Balkus's theorem holds empirically.} As-IID splitting matches or beats K$^2$-fold across two DGPs, three learners, and multiple sample sizes.
  \item \textbf{Whether you need cross-fitting depends on the learner, not the data structure.} MARS and Lasso don't need it (they don't overfit). Deep RF is catastrophic without it. Both as-IID and Chiang fix it equally.
  \item \textbf{The real issue is standard errors, not splitting.} When cluster sizes grow with the sample, coverage drops for both strategies --- the cluster-robust SE formula breaks down.
  \item \textbf{Software should separate splitting from variance estimation.} DoubleML bundles them, preventing the optimal combination: as-IID splitting + cluster-robust SEs.
  \item \textbf{Practical advice:} Use as-IID cross-fitting. Always use cluster-robust standard errors. If clusters are large, consider bootstrap alternatives.
\end{enumerate}
