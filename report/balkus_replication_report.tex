\section{Background}
%======================================================================

In causal machine learning, \emph{cross-fitting} eliminates empirical process (EP) bias from flexible nuisance estimation \citep{chernozhukov2018}. For clustered data, \citet{chiang2021} propose $K^2$-fold splitting: partition row and column clusters into $K$ groups, yielding $K^2$ test blocks with training folds that exclude all correlated observations. \citet{balkus2026} prove this elaborate splitting is \textbf{typically unnecessary}: standard as-IID splitting --- ignoring clustering --- eliminates EP bias whenever cluster sizes are bounded (their Corollary~2). Crucially, they note that ``variance estimates must still be adjusted to account for inter-unit correlation.''

\paragraph{Research questions.}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em, nosep]
  \item Can we reproduce Balkus's finding that as-IID $\geq$ Chiang K$^2$-fold in Python?
  \item Does it hold under harder DGPs that violate Balkus's ``safety nets''?
  \item Where does the bounded-cluster assumption break, and what fails first?
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Three strategies.} \textbf{no\_cf}: no splitting (baseline). \textbf{as\_iid}: standard $K$-fold, ignoring clusters. \textbf{chiang}: $K^2$-fold two-way splitting \citep[Algorithm~1]{chiang2021}.


%======================================================================
\section{Evaluation: Morris et al.\ (2019)}
\label{sec:morris}
%======================================================================

We follow the ADEMP framework of \citet{morris2019}. Each replication yields a point estimate $\hat{\theta}_i$ and a formula-based SE $\widehat{SE}_i$. The key diagnostic is:

\begin{equation}
  \text{RelSE} = \frac{\text{ModSE}}{\text{EmpSE}} = \frac{\sqrt{\frac{1}{n_\text{sim}} \sum_i \widehat{SE}_i^{\,2}}}{\text{SD}(\hat{\theta}_1, \ldots, \hat{\theta}_{n_\text{sim}})}
\end{equation}

\noindent where EmpSE is the true sampling variability (SD of estimates across replications) and ModSE is the RMS of formula-based SEs. RelSE $\approx 1$ means well-calibrated SEs; $<1$ is anti-conservative (dangerous); $>1$ is over-conservative (wasteful). Unlike coverage, RelSE reveals \emph{how far off} the SEs are and \emph{in which direction}.

\section{Balkus Replication}
\label{sec:balkus}
%======================================================================

\subsection{MARS Doesn't Need Cross-Fitting}

We test three learners on Balkus's two-way clustered AIPW DGP: MARS (R's \texttt{earth} via \texttt{rpy2}), Deep RF (sklearn, fully-grown trees), and LASSO. Four DGP variants progressively remove ``safety nets'': Original (direct R replication), No Compression (extreme propensity weights), Shared Effects (maximal cross-fold leakage), and Adversarial (sparse bipartite graph).

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{replications/balkus/results/plots/mars_vs_deep_rf.png}
\caption{MARS bias is near zero with or without cross-fitting; Deep RF bias only reaches zero \emph{with} cross-fitting.}
\label{fig:mars-vs-rf}
\end{figure}

MARS achieves 89--96\% coverage \textbf{without any cross-fitting}. Its GCV pruning makes it approximately Donsker-class \citep[p.~C16]{chernozhukov2018}. This means MARS \emph{cannot} test Balkus's theorem --- we cannot distinguish ``Theorem~1 works'' from ``cross-fitting is irrelevant.'' Deep RF ($\texttt{max\_depth}=\infty$) memorizes training data, creating exactly the EP bias that cross-fitting eliminates. The Donsker diagnostic confirms: MARS decays at $n^{-1/2}$ without CF; Deep RF does not (Figure~\ref{fig:donsker}).

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{replications/balkus/results/plots/donsker_diagnostic.png}
\caption{Log-log bias decay without CF. MARS achieves the Donsker rate; Deep RF does not.}
\label{fig:donsker}
\end{figure}


\subsection{As-IID Wins Everywhere}

Across all DGPs, learners, and sample sizes ($N \in \{20,30,50\}$, 200 replications each): \textbf{as-IID matches or beats Chiang K$^2$-fold.} Deep RF no\_cf is catastrophic (0.8\% coverage on Shared Effects). Both strategies rescue it to 88--96\%. As-IID has consistently lower RMSE because Chiang discards $\sim$75\% of training data per fold.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{replications/balkus/results/plots/scorecard_heatmap.png}
\caption{Coverage difference (as-IID $-$ Chiang). Blue = as-IID better.}
\end{figure}


\subsection{Morris RelSE: IID vs Cluster-Robust SEs}
\label{sec:balkus-relse}

Coverage alone hides whether SEs are ``slightly off'' or ``fundamentally wrong.'' We compute RelSE separately for IID and cluster-robust (CR) SEs.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{replications/balkus/results/plots/morris_relse.png}
\caption{\textbf{Morris RelSE.} (A)~Most points sit above the diagonal: CR SEs are better calibrated than IID. Red no-CF points cluster in the anti-conservative corner. (B)~Deep RF no\_cf: RelSE $= 0.39$ (IID) / $0.58$ (CR) --- both catastrophic. With CF, both reach $\approx 1.0$.}
\label{fig:morris-relse}
\end{figure}

\begin{table}[H]
\centering\small
\caption{Mean RelSE by learner $\times$ strategy, averaged over DGPs with CR SEs available.}
\label{tab:relse}
\begin{tabular}{llrrrr}
\toprule
\textbf{Learner} & \textbf{Strategy} & \textbf{RelSE (IID)} & \textbf{RelSE (CR)} & \textbf{$\Delta$} & \textbf{Cov.\ CR} \\
\midrule
\multirow{3}{*}{MARS}    & no\_cf  & 0.83 & \textbf{0.87} & $+$0.04 & 88.7\% \\
                          & as\_iid & 0.90 & \textbf{0.90} & $+$0.00 & 94.5\% \\
                          & chiang  & 0.84 & \textbf{0.90} & $+$0.06 & 95.5\% \\
\midrule
\multirow{3}{*}{Deep RF}  & no\_cf  & \textbf{0.39} & 0.58 & $+$0.19 & 24.5\% \\
                          & as\_iid & 0.99 & \textbf{0.98} & $-$0.01 & 91.0\% \\
                          & chiang  & 0.97 & \textbf{0.97} & $+$0.00 & 92.5\% \\
\midrule
\multirow{3}{*}{LASSO}   & no\_cf  & 0.90 & \textbf{0.95} & $+$0.05 & 90.0\% \\
                          & as\_iid & 0.93 & \textbf{0.97} & $+$0.04 & 93.5\% \\
                          & chiang  & 0.93 & \textbf{0.97} & $+$0.04 & 93.8\% \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\paragraph{Key insight: two independent corrections.}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em, nosep]
  \item \textbf{Cross-fitting fixes bias}: Deep RF RelSE jumps from 0.39 to 0.99 --- the influence function is now unbiased.
  \item \textbf{CR SEs fix variance}: consistent 3--5\% RelSE improvement over IID SEs across all learners.
  \item Without CF, \emph{no variance estimator can save you} --- the influence function itself is contaminated.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Head-to-Head on Chiang's Own DGP}

Neither paper performs this comparison. Chiang only tests K$^2$-fold vs no-CF; Balkus uses a different estimator. We fill the gap using the partially linear IV DGP from \citet[Section~4.1]{chiang2021}, with our custom estimator that \textbf{decouples} cross-fitting strategy from variance estimator.

\subsection{DoubleML Validation: The Decoupling Revelation}

We repeated the comparison using the \textbf{official DoubleML library} (v0.11.1) with its built-in DGP. Parameters: $N\!=\!M\!=\!25$, $p\!=\!100$, $K\!=\!3$, LassoCV, 100 replications.

\begin{tabular}{lrrrrrr}
\toprule
\textbf{Strategy} & \textbf{Bias} & \textbf{EmpSE} & \textbf{ModSE} & \textbf{RelSE} & \textbf{RMSE} & \textbf{Cov.} \\
\midrule
Chiang K$^2$-fold & $+$0.007 & 0.074 & 0.106 & 1.43 & 0.074 & 100.0\% \\

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{replications/chiang/results/doubleml_comparison.png}
\caption{DoubleML diagnostic. As-IID has tighter estimates but anti-conservative SEs (RelSE $=0.66$). Chiang has over-conservative SEs (RelSE $=1.43$, 100\% coverage but uninformative CIs).}
\label{fig:doubleml}

This reveals a \textbf{software design flaw}: DoubleML bundles two independent choices into \texttt{cluster\_cols}:

\begin{center}\small
\begin{tabular}{lcc}
\toprule
\texttt{cluster\_cols} & \textbf{Cross-fitting} & \textbf{Variance estimator} \\
\midrule
Set     & K$^2$-fold & Cluster-robust \\
Not set & As-IID     & IID \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\noindent But Balkus's Theorem~1 shows these are logically independent. The optimal combination is:
\begin{center}
\fbox{\textbf{As-IID splitting} + \textbf{Cluster-robust SE} = best of both worlds}
\end{center}


%======================================================================
\section{Boundary: Where Corollary~2 Breaks}
\label{sec:boundary}
%======================================================================

A full $N \times M$ grid violates Corollary~2 (cluster degree grows with $N$). With strong cluster effects and clean proxies, coverage declines as the grid grows --- from 85\% to 71\% for \emph{both} strategies. Neither reaches 95\%. The failure is the CGM variance estimator, not cross-fitting. On bounded-cluster adversarial DGPs (sparse bipartite graph, $d=20$), both strategies achieve 97\% coverage with CR SEs, confirming the boundary is exactly where the theory predicts.


%======================================================================
\section{Conclusion}
%======================================================================

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em, nosep]
  \item \textbf{Balkus et al.\ are empirically robust.} As-IID matches or beats K$^2$-fold across five DGPs, three learners, multiple sample sizes.
  \item \textbf{Learner choice $>$ fold strategy.} MARS doesn't need CF; Deep RF is catastrophic without it. Both strategies fix it equally.
  \item \textbf{The boundary is variance estimation, not splitting.} When Corollary~2 breaks, coverage declines for both strategies equally.
  \item \textbf{Software must decouple splitting from variance.} DoubleML's bundled toggle prevents the optimal combination.
  \item \textbf{Recommendation:} as-IID cross-fitting + cluster-robust SEs.
\end{enumerate}
