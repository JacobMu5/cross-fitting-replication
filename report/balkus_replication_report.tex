\section{Background}
%======================================================================

When we use machine learning to estimate causal effects, the ML model can overfit: it learns patterns in the training data that don't generalize. This creates \emph{empirical process (EP) bias} --- a systematic error in the treatment effect estimate caused by using the same data for both nuisance estimation and inference. \emph{Cross-fitting} fixes this by splitting the data in two --- train the ML model on one half, estimate the causal effect on the other half, then swap \citep{chernozhukov2018}.

But what if units in the data are correlated --- e.g., students nested in schools, or workers nested in firms? \citet{chiang2021} argue that splits must \emph{respect} the clustering: if two observations share a cluster, they cannot be in different folds. They propose K$^2$-fold splitting, which partitions both row and column clusters into $K$ groups. The downside: with $K=2$, each training fold sees only $\sim$25\% of the data.

\citet{balkus2026} prove this elaborate splitting is \textbf{usually unnecessary}. Their Theorem~1 shows that plain random splitting --- ignoring clusters entirely --- still eliminates the EP bias, as long as cluster sizes don't grow with the sample. They call this ``as-IID'' splitting.

There is one caveat. Balkus note that even though the \emph{splitting strategy} doesn't need to know about clusters, the \emph{standard errors} still do:
\begin{quote}
\emph{``Variance estimates used for the construction of confidence intervals must be adjusted to account for inter-unit correlation.''} --- \citet[Section~4]{balkus2026}
\end{quote}

This distinction --- splitting strategy vs.\ standard error formula --- is the central theme of this report.

\paragraph{Three strategies we compare.}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em, nosep]
  \item \textbf{No CF}: No splitting at all. Train and predict on the same data. This is the baseline that shows when cross-fitting matters.
  \item \textbf{As-IID}: Standard random $K$-fold splitting, ignoring clusters.
  \item \textbf{Chiang K$^2$-fold}: Cluster-aware splitting that guarantees statistical independence between training and test data.
\end{itemize}

\section{How We Measure Success}
\label{sec:morris}
%======================================================================

We run each experiment 200 times with different random seeds. Each run produces two numbers: a treatment effect estimate $\hat{\theta}$ and a standard error $\widehat{SE}$ (what the formula \emph{thinks} the uncertainty is). Because we control the data-generating process, we know the true treatment effect $\theta_0$.

Following \citet{morris2019}, we compute:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em, nosep]
  \item \textbf{Bias}: How far off are the estimates on average? ($\bar{\hat{\theta}} - \theta_0$)
  \item \textbf{EmpSE}: The \emph{true} uncertainty, measured as the standard deviation of the 200 estimates.
  \item \textbf{ModSE}: The \emph{reported} uncertainty, computed as $\sqrt{\text{mean}(\widehat{SE}^2)}$ across the 200 runs.
  \item \textbf{RelSE = ModSE / EmpSE}: Are the reported standard errors correct?
  \item \textbf{Coverage}: How often does the 95\% confidence interval contain the true $\theta_0$?
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Why RelSE matters more than coverage.} Two methods can both have 88\% coverage, but one might have RelSE $= 0.90$ (SEs are slightly too small --- fixable) while the other has RelSE $= 0.39$ (SEs are catastrophically wrong --- the method is fundamentally broken). Coverage hides this difference. RelSE reveals it.

\section{Balkus Replication: Which Learner Needs Cross-Fitting?}
\label{sec:balkus}
%======================================================================

We test three ML learners on Balkus's two-way clustered DGP. Each learner sits at a different point on the ``overfitting spectrum'':

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em, nosep]
  \item \textbf{MARS} (R's \texttt{earth}): Additive splines with built-in pruning. A ``careful'' learner that avoids overfitting by design.
  \item \textbf{Deep RF} (sklearn): Fully-grown random forests that memorize the training data. A ``reckless'' learner --- exactly the kind cross-fitting was invented for.
  \item \textbf{Lasso}: L1-regularized regression. Another ``careful'' learner, like MARS.
\end{itemize}

We also go beyond Balkus's original DGP. Their simulation has several ``safety nets'' that make life easy for the estimator. We remove them one by one to stress-test the theory:

\subsection{The Key Finding: MARS Doesn't Need Cross-Fitting At All}

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{replications/balkus/results/plots/mars_vs_deep_rf.png}
\caption{MARS bias is near zero regardless of splitting strategy. Deep RF bias is large without cross-fitting but disappears with either as-IID or Chiang splitting.}
\label{fig:mars-vs-rf}
\end{figure}

MARS achieves 89--96\% coverage \emph{without any cross-fitting}. Its built-in GCV pruning prevents the overfitting that creates EP bias. As \citet[p.~C16]{chernozhukov2018} note: if the learner belongs to a ``Donsker class'' (roughly: it doesn't overfit enough to create EP bias), cross-fitting is unnecessary.

This creates a problem: \textbf{MARS is the wrong learner to test Balkus's theorem.} If cross-fitting doesn't matter regardless, we can't tell whether as-IID splitting ``works'' or whether splitting is just irrelevant. That's why we introduced Deep RF --- a learner where cross-fitting genuinely matters.

\paragraph{Deep RF confirms Balkus.} Without cross-fitting, Deep RF creates massive EP bias: coverage drops to 0.8--45\%. With \emph{either} as-IID or Chiang splitting, the EP bias vanishes and coverage recovers to 88--96\%. The two strategies perform equally well. As-IID has lower RMSE because Chiang discards $\sim$75\% of training data per fold.


\subsection{The SE Calibration Story}

Coverage tells you \emph{whether} your confidence intervals work. RelSE tells you \emph{why} they fail. We compute RelSE separately for two types of standard errors: IID (ignoring clusters) and cluster-robust (CR, accounting for clusters).

\paragraph{What this table shows:}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em, nosep]
  \item \textbf{Deep RF without CF is broken.} RelSE $= 0.39$ means the reported SEs are only 39\% of the true uncertainty. The confidence intervals are far too narrow. Even switching to cluster-robust SEs (0.58) doesn't fix it --- the EP bias contaminates the influence function \emph{before} the SE formula even runs.
  \item \textbf{Cross-fitting fixes it.} With either as-IID or Chiang, RelSE jumps to $\sim$0.98. Once the EP bias is eliminated, the SE formula receives an uncontaminated influence function.
  \item \textbf{CR SEs add a small but consistent improvement} of 3--5\% over IID SEs, across all learners and strategies. This confirms Balkus's caveat: you need cluster-robust SEs regardless of how you split.
  \item \textbf{MARS and Lasso don't need cross-fitting for EP bias.} Their RelSE is already 0.83--0.95 without CF (they're Donsker-class). But they still benefit from CR SEs.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Bottom line:} Cross-fitting and cluster-robust SEs fix two \emph{different} problems. Cross-fitting eliminates the EP bias (Chernozhukov's contribution). CR SEs correct the variance underestimation from ignoring within-cluster correlation (Cameron et al.'s contribution). Both are needed independently.

\section{Chiang Replication: Testing on Chiang's Own DGP}
\label{sec:chiang}
%======================================================================

Everything above uses Balkus's AIPW estimator. A natural question: do the results hold on a completely different estimator?

We replicate the partially linear IV (PLIV) model from \citet[Section~4.1]{chiang2021}. This is their own simulation design with Lasso as the first-stage learner and $p = 100$ covariates, so it's the fairest possible test. Lasso, like MARS, is approximately Donsker --- it doesn't create large EP bias. The Chiang replication therefore tests \emph{data starvation} and \emph{decoupling} rather than EP bias removal.

\paragraph{Key findings:}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em, nosep]
  \item \textbf{CR SEs improve coverage by 7--20 percentage points} over IID SEs. Within-cluster correlation is real and IID SEs ignore it.
  \item \textbf{As-IID + CR SE wins}: 92.7\% at $(25,25)$, 92.3\% at $(50,50)$.
  \item \textbf{K$^2$-fold suffers from data starvation}: with $K=2$ on a $25 \times 25$ grid, each fold trains on only $\sim$144 observations for 100 covariates. As-IID uses all 625.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{The remaining 8\% gap.} Our CR SEs use the classic CGM (2011) formula, which is known to be slightly downward-biased when the number of clusters is moderate ($G = 25$). Newer bias-corrected estimators (CR2, Bell \& McCaffrey, 2002) would likely close this gap. For our argument --- that as-IID + CR SE dominates K$^2$-fold regardless of SE formula --- the conclusion is already clear.

As-IID has \textbf{25\% lower variance}, but \textbf{neither strategy produces valid confidence intervals}:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em, nosep]
  \item \textbf{Chiang: 99.5\% coverage, RelSE $= 1.23$.} SEs are 23\% too large --- you cover everything but learn nothing.
  \item \textbf{As-IID: 82\% coverage, RelSE $= 0.71$.} SEs are 29\% too small. DoubleML uses IID SEs when \texttt{cluster\_cols} is not set.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{The problem is software design.} DoubleML bundles two independent choices into one toggle:

\begin{center}\small
\begin{tabular}{lcc}
\toprule
\texttt{cluster\_cols} & \textbf{Splitting} & \textbf{Standard errors} \\
\midrule
Set     & K$^2$-fold (cluster-aware) & Cluster-robust \\
Not set & As-IID (random)            & IID \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Balkus's theorem tells us these are \emph{logically independent} choices. You want as-IID splitting (maximize training data) \emph{with} cluster-robust SEs (account for correlation). DoubleML doesn't allow this. Our implementation does, and it achieves the best coverage (Table~\ref{tab:chiang-decoupling}).

\section{Where the Theory Breaks}
\label{sec:boundary}
%======================================================================

Balkus's Corollary~2 requires that cluster sizes are \emph{bounded} --- each row contains at most $C$ columns, where $C$ doesn't grow with the sample. In a full $N \times M$ grid, every row contains $M$ observations, so $C = M \to \infty$. We tested this with strong cluster effects. Coverage declines from 85\% to 71\% as the grid grows --- for \emph{both} strategies equally. The culprit is the cluster-robust SE formula itself (which assumes fixed cluster sizes), not the splitting strategy. On bounded-cluster DGPs (sparse bipartite graph, $d=20$), both strategies achieve 97\% coverage, confirming the boundary is exactly where the theory predicts.


%======================================================================
\section{Conclusion}
%======================================================================

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em, nosep]
  \item \textbf{Balkus's theorem holds empirically.} As-IID splitting matches or beats K$^2$-fold across five DGPs, three learners, and multiple sample sizes.
  \item \textbf{Whether you need cross-fitting depends on the learner, not the data structure.} MARS and Lasso don't need it (they don't overfit). Deep RF is catastrophic without it. Both as-IID and Chiang fix it equally.
  \item \textbf{The real issue is standard errors, not splitting.} When cluster sizes grow with the sample, coverage drops for both strategies --- the cluster-robust SE formula breaks down.
  \item \textbf{Software should separate splitting from variance estimation.} DoubleML bundles them, preventing the optimal combination: as-IID splitting + cluster-robust SEs.
  \item \textbf{Practical advice:} Use as-IID cross-fitting. Always use cluster-robust standard errors. If clusters are large, consider bootstrap alternatives.
\end{enumerate}
